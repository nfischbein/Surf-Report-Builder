Data sources

Where the data comes from.

Most AI surf prompts can't check live data. They rely on whatever the model already knows — which is usually months out of date — or on web searches that often pull stale cached pages.

We take a different approach. When your reports run, they pull from a small server we maintain that fetches data live from official sources, server-side, before your AI sees it. No commercial forecasts. No interpretation. Just the actual numbers.

  • NOAA buoys Live wave height, period, direction, and water temperature.
  • NOAA tide stations Real-time water level and tide predictions.
  • FAA airport weather (METAR) Current wind observations from the airports nearest your break.
  • National Weather Service forecasts Upcoming wind for your session window.

Every reading is timestamped. Every source is named in the report. If a sensor is offline, the report says so instead of guessing.

FAQ

Questions worth asking.

Which AI should I use?

Claude.ai with web search enabled is the recommended runtime. It's what we test against and what handles live data lookups most reliably.

ChatGPT with browsing should also work but we haven't formally tested it yet. Avoid free-tier ChatGPT (no web access) and Perplexity (returns aggressively cached data that can be months out of date).

What does this cost?

Nothing from us. Everything here is free. You'll need an AI subscription if your AI of choice charges for web access — most do — but that's between you and the AI provider.

Do I need to know how to code?

No. If you can copy text, paste it into your AI, and answer a few questions about your break, you can use this.

Why doesn't it just tell me whether to surf today?

Because nobody but you knows what's worth surfing for you. Crowd tolerance, board choice, skill level, what you're trying to work on, whether you have an hour or three — all of it matters, and none of it is something a forecast can know.

The reports describe what's likely to happen at your break in plain English: size, shape, tide, wind, who the session favors, and what could make the call wrong. You make the go/no-go call.

Why don't you use Surfline data?

Surfline and similar commercial sites mix raw data with editorial interpretation — ratings, predicted faces, "go" calls, stylized summaries. The system is built to ignore all of that and rely only on the underlying official sources (NOAA, NWS, FAA), which are what Surfline itself ultimately pulls from.

The goal is reports built on raw data and your local knowledge, not on someone else's marketing call.

What if there's no buoy near my break?

Most coastal breaks have a usable buoy somewhere nearby — often farther offshore than you'd expect. The Master Prompt's research step looks for the closest usable stations and builds a local model that accounts for the distance and direction.

Breaks in genuinely instrument-poor areas (some remote stretches of coast) will produce reports with lower confidence, which the system labels honestly rather than hides.

Is my data going anywhere?

The Master Prompt itself doesn't send anything anywhere. Your conversation with your AI stays between you and your AI provider — the same as any other AI conversation.

The optional Tracking system stores your scores in a Google Sheet that you create and own. Nothing is sent to us. Nothing is shared with other users.

What does the optional Tracking actually do?

After you surf, you log a quick score: how close reality felt to what the report predicted, on a scale of -2 to +2. Over time those scores build a personal track record.

Eventually the system can use that history to flag patterns ("similar long-period south swells at this tide have averaged -0.7 in your archive") and adjust confidence accordingly. It's a longer-term feature; the value compounds with every session you log.
